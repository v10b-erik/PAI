## Response

**Guillermo Rauch:**

This is the question that makes or breaks companies. Let me be very clear about this.

**Don't build for enterprise. Not yet. Maybe not ever directly.**

Here's what I learned building Vercel: We never sold to enterprise. We built something developers loved so much that they dragged it into their enterprises. The CTO didn't buy Vercel—the developer who was 10x more productive with it made the CTO buy it.

Enterprise sales is a trap for early-stage infra companies:
- 18-month sales cycles
- Feature requests that derail your roadmap
- Security questionnaires longer than your codebase
- Procurement processes designed in 1997

You'll burn runway chasing logos instead of building product.

**Build for AI-native companies. Specifically:**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                  IDEAL CUSTOMER PROFILE                 │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                         │
│  Stage: Series A to C                                   │
│  Size: 20-500 employees                                 │
│  Industry: AI/ML-first companies                        │
│                                                         │
│  Examples:                                              │
│  • AI agent companies (need low-latency everywhere)    │
│  • Robotics startups (factory floor networks)          │
│  • Computer vision companies (edge inference)          │
│  • AI dev tools (dogfood your own infra)              │
│  • Autonomous vehicle companies (can't afford drops)   │
│                                                         │
│  Why they're perfect:                                   │
│  ✓ They GET the problem viscerally                     │
│  ✓ They move fast (days, not quarters)                 │

│  ✓ They have real edge/office deployments              │

│                                                         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**But here's the real insight:**

The AI-native companies are just your *early adopters*. The actual market is **every company becoming AI-native**.

In 3 years, the insurance company, the hospital, the retailer—they all have AI agents, edge inference, machine-to-machine traffic. They'll all need what you're building. But they won't buy it from a startup today. They'll buy it when:

1. The AI-native companies prove the model
2. Their own AI initiatives hit network walls
3. You have case studies and credibility

**The wedge strategy:**

```
Year 1:  AI-native startups (they find you)
         └── Ship fast, learn, iterate
         └── Build reference customers
         └── Prove the platform
         
Year 2:  AI-forward mid-market (they hear about you)
         └── Tech companies going AI-first
         └── Startups that scaled up
         └── Partner channel begins
         
Year 3+: Enterprise (they need you)
         └── Their AI initiatives demand it
         └── Your early customers are now big
         └── You're the proven solution
```

**One more thing:**

There's a segment everyone ignores: **AI infrastructure locations**.

- Data centers adding edge compute
- Co-working spaces with AI company tenants  
- Smart buildings with AI systems
- Manufacturing facilities going autonomous

These aren't the *users* of AI—they're the *hosts*. They need AI-ready networking as a feature of their space. That's a B2B2B play worth exploring.

**The bottom line:**

Build for people who already feel the pain, move fast, and will tell others. That's AI-native companies at Series A-C. They're not a stepping stone to enterprise—they ARE the new enterprise in 5 years.

Don't chase yesterday's customers. Build for tomorrow's and let them grow into the market.
